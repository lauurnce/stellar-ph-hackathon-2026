# Pangolin — Pitch Narrative & Storytelling Strategy
## Stellar Philippines Hackathon 2026 | May 2026

---

## The Hero's Journey Structure

### Lead with Maria, Not Blockchain

Maria is a 24-year-old illustrator from Cebu. Takes commissions on Twitter and Instagram. Charges ₱3,500 per character illustration — about 20 hours of work. No contract lawyer. No LLC. Has Canva, a drawing tablet, and PayPal.

This is your protagonist. She is not a persona — she is someone most Filipinos in that room know personally or are adjacent to.

---

## The 5-Minute Narrative Arc

### [0:00–0:30] — Emotional Hook (DO NOT start with "Hi, we are Team Pangolin")

Open with the story, not the introduction:

> "Imagine you spend three weeks drawing someone's dream character. You pour your style, your craft, your late nights into it. You send the final file. The client loves it — they say thank you. Then, two weeks later, you get an email from PayPal. Chargeback filed. Case closed. You lose ₱3,500, you lose the work, and you owe PayPal ₱900 just for the privilege of losing."
>
> "This happened to over 40,000 Filipino freelancers last year. And PayPal's own terms say — this is by design."

Stop. Let that land. One beat of silence. Then say your names.

---

### [0:30–1:30] — Build the Tension

- Show PayPal's ACTUAL policy language on screen — verbatim, not paraphrased. Nothing is more damning than the company's own words.
- Power imbalance: clients KNOW this. Some exploit it intentionally.
- Scale: 1.5M Philippine registered freelancers. Growing sector. Structural vulnerability, not niche problem.
- Humanize the fee: ₱900 = roughly one day's minimum wage in Metro Manila. They lose work, payment, AND a day's wages just to officially lose.

---

### [1:30–2:00] — Status Quo Failure

Why existing tools fail (prevent "why not just use X?" from judges):

| Tool | Why It Fails Maria |
|---|---|
| PayPal | The problem IS PayPal. Actively hostile to digital goods sellers. |
| Wise/GCash | Payment rails only. No escrow, no dispute mechanism. |
| Upwork/Fiverr | 20% fees. Clients are on Twitter, not Upwork. Dispute systems favor buyers. |
| Contracts/NDAs | Unenforceable across international borders without expensive lawyers. ₱3,500 commission ≠ ₱15,000 in legal fees. |

The gap: No tool built for informal, cross-border, small-ticket digital commission economy. Not one.

---

### [2:00–3:00] — Introduce Pangolin

Don't say "we built an app." Say what changed in Maria's world:

> "With Pangolin, here is Maria's new reality. The client deposits ₱3,500 worth of USDC into a Soroban smart contract on Stellar. Maria sees the funds locked on-chain — not a promise, not a screenshot, not a PayPal hold. Cryptographic proof. She starts work. When she delivers, the client approves. Payment arrives in her wallet in 3 seconds. Fee: less than one centavo."

Then the key innovation:

> "But here is what makes Pangolin different from every other escrow tool. The client cannot get 100% back. Ever. Before work starts, both parties agree on a minimum payment percentage — say, 30%. That percentage is locked into the contract by code, not by policy. Not by our customer service team. Not by a judge. By math. Maria is guaranteed ₱1,050 minimum, even in the worst case. The chargeback scam simply does not work anymore."

**Live demo here if possible.** Even 30-second walkthrough of UI > five slides.

---

### [3:00–4:00] — Technical Credibility

Stellar ecosystem judges respect precision over hype. Be specific:

- **Soroban** — Stellar's native smart contract platform. Not a layer 2, not a wrapper. Native.
- **USDC on Stellar** — Circle-issued. Dollar-pegged. Same stablecoin major institutions use. No volatility risk on agreed payment amount.
- **3-5 second finality** — Compare to PayPal's 3-5 business day dispute resolution window.
- **$0.001 per transaction** — On ₱10,000 commission: PayPal takes ₱470+. Pangolin takes ₱0.056.
- **On-chain transparency** — Every escrow state (funded, locked, delivered, released, disputed) = immutable record. The "proof of delivery" PayPal cannot provide.

---

### [4:00–4:45] — Business Case

Keep tight. Show clear thinking, not inflated numbers:

- TAM: 1.5M Filipino freelancers. 1% adoption at avg ₱5,000 commission = ₱75M annual escrow volume.
- Revenue: 1% platform fee, client-side, successful releases only. Incentive = aligned with successful transactions.
- GTM: Filipino commission community on Twitter/X, DeviantArt, Cara.app. Existing informal reputation systems. Pangolin adds financial layer to trust that already exists socially.

---

### [4:45–5:00] — The Close (Make It Memorable)

DON'T end with "Thank you, any questions?" End with a statement:

> "PayPal's policy says digital work has no proof of delivery. We disagree. Every stroke, every revision, every file transfer — it is all on-chain now. Pangolin does not ask clients to be honest. It makes dishonesty structurally impossible."

Then: team names, project handle, one call to action (QR code to demo / landing page).

## Problem Statement — Three Versions

### Version 1: One Sentence (Social / Intro slide)
> "Filipino digital artists lose their work and their payment to PayPal chargebacks because digital commissions are explicitly excluded from seller protection — and there has never been a tool built to fix that."

**Punchier Twitter version:**
> "PayPal's own policy says digital art has no proof of delivery. 40,000 Filipino freelancers found out the hard way."

---

### Version 2: One Paragraph (Pitch Deck Slide 2)
> The Philippines has over 1.5 million freelancers, many of them digital artists taking commissions online. Their clients pay via PayPal. When a client files a chargeback after receiving the work, PayPal sides with them automatically — because PayPal explicitly excludes digital and intangible services from its Seller Protection program. No tracking number, no protection. The freelancer loses the payment, loses the delivered artwork, and pays a ₱900 dispute fee for the privilege. Existing platforms like Upwork charge 20% fees and require clients to already be on the platform. Contracts are unenforceable across borders without expensive lawyers. There is currently no financial tool designed for the informal, cross-border, small-ticket digital commission economy. That gap is what Pangolin fills.

---

### Version 3: 2-Minute Verbal (Live Presentation)
*(Designed to be spoken. Conversational pacing.)*

> "Let me tell you about a problem that most people in this room have either experienced personally or watched a friend go through.
>
> You are a digital artist. You are good at what you do. A client finds you on Twitter — maybe they saw your work, maybe a friend recommended you. They want a commission. You agree on ₱4,000 for a character illustration. You send your payment details. They pay via PayPal. You spend two weeks on this piece. You are proud of it. You send the final file. The client says they love it. Case closed, right?
>
> Two weeks later. You get an email. PayPal Dispute Center. Unauthorized transaction claim. And here is the part that will make your stomach drop: PayPal sides with the client. Automatically. Not after an investigation. Automatically. Because PayPal's Seller Protection program — and you can read this yourself on their website — explicitly excludes digital goods and intangible services. No tracking number, no protection.
>
> You just lost the ₱4,000. You lost the artwork — the client keeps it, by the way. And you owe PayPal a ₱900 dispute processing fee. For losing. That is what the current system looks like for 1.5 million Filipino freelancers.
>
> We spent a long time asking: why is there no escrow tool that actually works for this community? And the answer is: nobody built one that understands the informal, cross-border, small-ticket nature of digital commissions. Until now."

## The Judge Test

### "Why blockchain? Why not just use PayPal escrow or Upwork?"

PayPal's escrow equivalent still excludes digital goods from seller protection — problem is in their policy layer, not tech. Upwork requires both parties on platform, charges 20%, most Filipino commission clients are on Twitter not Upwork.

Blockchain specifically: the smart contract IS the escrow agent. No customer service that can be social-engineered. No policy that can be updated to exclude you. Minimum payment guarantee enforced by mathematics neither party can override — including us. No centralized platform can make that guarantee honestly because they always retain ability to reverse decisions.

---

### "Why Stellar specifically?"

Three concrete reasons:

1. **Soroban is native** — not bridging to Ethereum, not using layer 2, not wrapping. Native means lower fees, tighter integration, no bridge risk.
2. **USDC on Stellar is Circle-issued** — regulated stablecoin, dollar-pegged. Payment value agreed in contract = payment value received. Volatile native token would create new risk; USDC eliminates it.
3. **3-5 second finality at $0.001** — On ₱3,500 commission, PayPal takes ₱175-200. Stellar takes ₱0.056. Meaningful when target user earns ₱15,000-25,000/month part-time.

---

### "What's your unfair advantage?"

**Three layers:**

1. **Community-first** — Building from inside Filipino digital art commission community. Cultural context (Twitter TOS posts, F2U/P2U bases, commission structure norms) cannot be copied quickly by outsiders.
2. **Minimum payment mechanic** — No competing platform has hard-floor minimum payout enforced at contract level. Genuine product innovation that changes dispute risk calculus.
3. **Soroban timing** — Soroban ecosystem is still early. Teams building now develop expertise head start over later entrants.

---

### "How do you make money?"

1% fee on successful transaction releases only. No fee on disputes, failed, or cancelled contracts.

This matters: incentive is aligned with successful transactions. We only make money when the freelancer gets paid. No financial reason to side with anyone in a dispute.

At ₱100M annual escrow volume (≈20,000 avg commissions/month) → ₱1M annually. Sustainable fee structure. Future expansion: milestone-based multi-release contracts, identity verification badges, B2B licensing to commission platforms, SEA expansion.

---

### "How do you stop someone from copying this?"

Be honest: Code is copyable. Smart contract logic can be forked.

What's NOT easily copied: community trust, network effects of freelancers/clients already on platform, reputation system built over time. First escrow platform to become default in Filipino commission community wins by network effects, not patent protection.

Practical: Stellar/Soroban-specific expertise, cultural fit with community, distribution relationships. Copy faces different technical tradeoffs on another chain AND the same distribution problem.

**Honest answer: moat = community + speed + trust, not code secrecy.**

---

### "What if client disputes in bad faith AND freelancer delivers bad work?"

Answer head-on.

Minimum percentage agreed upfront before work begins. Poor work delivered + client disputes → minimum % still releases to freelancer, client gets remainder. Neither party can force other to release/withhold more than agreed split.

Is this perfect? No. We cannot judge quality on-chain. Design philosophy is deliberate: ensure neither party walks away with nothing.

Future roadmap: optional third-party arbitration layer where trusted community members (established artists with reputation scores) review evidence and recommend split adjustment. Not in V1, but contract architecture supports it.

## Emotional vs. Rational Balance

### The Rule of Thirds

| Pitch Segment | Tone |
|---|---|
| First third | Pure emotion. Maria's story. The injustice. The human cost. **Don't mention Soroban yet.** |
| Middle third | The pivot. Introduce solution emotionally first ("here's what changed for Maria"), then technically ("here's how we built it"). |
| Final third | Pure credibility. Technical stack, business model, market size. Speak Stellar engineer language. |

### The Bridge Sentence (Serves Both Audiences)

> "Maria does not know what Soroban is. She does not need to. She sees a wallet address, she sees a locked balance, she starts work. The technology is invisible. The protection is real."

- Emotional judges hear: accessible, human-centered design
- Technical judges hear: good UX means abstracting complexity correctly

### Translating Numbers to Lives

"1.5 million freelancers" = abstract.

"The average Filipino commission artist takes 4–6 commissions per month at ₱3,000–₱5,000 each. One successful chargeback scam wipes out 25% of their monthly income." = concrete.

Make judges feel scale before they process it analytically.

## The One-Liner — Five Variations

| Variation | Line | Evaluation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Your work deserves a lock. Not a promise." | Concise. "Lock" = literal + emotional. Attacks PayPal without naming them. Slight weakness: abstract without context. |
| 2 | "Pangolin makes it structurally impossible to steal from Filipino freelancers." | Bold. "Steal" is accurate and defensible. "Structurally impossible" signals tech sophistication without jargon. Better as closing statement than tagline. |
| 3 | "Get paid. Always. Enforced by code." | Three fragments. Punchy. Distills core value in 5 words. Warning: "always" is technically inaccurate (minimum, not 100%) — probe risk with judges. |
| 4 | "Built for every artist who ever got the chargeback email." | High community resonance. Too narrow for judges who haven't experienced it. Use in community marketing, not pitch. |
| **5 (RECOMMENDED)** | **"PayPal protects the buyer. Pangolin protects the work."** | Clean contrast. Names the enemy without aggression. "Protects the work" = freelancer's payment AND creative process integrity. Works at every familiarity level. |

**Primary tagline: Variation 5**
**Closing statement: Variation 2**
**Solution slide headline: Variation 3**

## What NOT to Say

| Don't Say | Why It Kills Trust | Say Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "We are disrupting PayPal." | Invites "how do you compete with a trillion-dollar company?" — unanswerable. | "We are serving a market PayPal abandoned." |
| "Blockchain ensures trust." | Cargo cult language. Means nothing to technical judges, annoys them. | Specific mechanism: "Smart contract holds funds in state neither party can unilaterally access until both conditions met." |
| Giant inflated TAM without sourcing | Judges spot this, damages credibility on everything else | Use real, defensible numbers for actual addressable market |
| "This is simple to build." | Undersells work, raises "why doesn't it exist already?" | "Technical implementation is tractable with Soroban — what was hard was designing dispute logic to be fair to both parties without a trusted third party." |
| Apologize for what's not built | Signals insecurity | Frame as roadmap: "V1 focuses on core flow. Arbitration layer is designed into contract architecture and is the next milestone." |
| "No one else is doing this." | Signals you haven't done competitive research | "Closest alternatives are [X], and here is the specific gap they leave." |
| Over-explaining Stellar to Stellar judges | They know what Soroban is. Condescending. | "Here's what we did with the tools" not "here's what the tools are." |
| End with "Thank you, any questions?" | Least memorable ending possible | End with reframe statement, then invite questions. Statement first. |
| Reading from slides | Signals discomfort with material | Slides = for audience. Words = for judges. Give pitch in power outage, lose nothing essential. |
| "Utilize" when you mean "use" | Overly formal = uncomfortable with material | "We lock the funds on-chain." Simple = confident. |

## Pre-Pitch Checklist

**48 hours before:**
- [ ] Every team member can explain minimum payment mechanic in one sentence without notes
- [ ] PayPal policy screenshot is current and accurate
- [ ] Live demo works on presentation laptop AND backup device
- [ ] Pitch timed at 4:30 (30 seconds of buffer)

**Morning of:**
- [ ] Run pitch once, out loud, standing up, in front of honest feedback giver
- [ ] Assign specific judge questions to specific team members
- [ ] QR code ready at two sizes: slide + printed backup

**In the room:**
- Start before fully settled — first sentence signals urgency
- If demo fails: don't apologize. "Let me walk you through what this looks like" → verbal walkthrough. How you handle failure matters more than whether it happens.
- After pitch, before Q&A: pause two full seconds. Own the room first.

## The Pangolin Metaphor (Use It)

The pangolin's shell is not armor — it is the only mammal that rolls into a perfect sphere when threatened, making it impossible to open by force.

That is the product. Not a wall. A design that makes the attack functionally useless.

Lead with that image if you want to. The metaphor earns itself.
